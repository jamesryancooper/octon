# The Resonant Computing Design Stack

Across all these pieces, a single through-line emerges: **technology should be convivial and resonant**. That is, it should expand people’s autonomy, understanding, and relationships, at human and societal scales, instead of extracting attention, data, or agency. Tools ought to feel like good neighbors and good instruments: small enough to understand, flexible enough to adapt, and principled enough to protect our rights and our time.

To make that concrete, we can fuse these manifestos and essays into a **Resonant Computing Design Stack (RCDS)**: a four-layer framework for designing and evaluating socio-technical systems. At the base is **Flourishing & Care (Ends)**: a commitment to human dignity, societal well-being, and ecological responsibility. On top of that sits **Structures & Scale**: incentives, ownership, and architectures that favor convivial, small, plural systems over hyper-scale extraction. Then comes **Attention & Interaction**: humane interfaces, calm technology, and transparent human–AI collaboration that align with human cognition and emotion. Finally, **Practice & Governance**: the craft methods, participatory processes, and oversight needed to keep systems accountable and evolving toward resonance.

Applied in practice, the RCDS tells us what to build more of—private, dedicated, plural, prosocial tools that people can truly own and understand—and what to avoid: dark patterns, addictive design, centralized surveillance, and “lab-rat” engagement metrics. It turns a stack of manifestos into a practical compass: for any product, you can ask, *Does this leave people more capable, coherent, and connected? Is it small enough, legible enough, and kind enough to trust?* If not, you have a clear map of where to change the architecture, incentives, or interface to move toward resonant computing.

## 1. Distilled core ideas by article

I’ll keep these tight and focus on what’s uniquely contributed.

### Ethical Design Manifesto – Small Technology Foundation

- **Thesis:** Technology should satisfy a hierarchy: **human rights → human effort → human experience**. Rights (privacy, decentralization, openness) are non-negotiable; experience (beauty, delight) comes *last*, not first.
- **Mental models:** A “hierarchy of needs” for tech; a checklist for ethical products.
- **Assumptions:** Individuals deserve strong privacy, autonomy, and legible systems; small, open, interoperable tools are the default shape of ethical tech.

---

### The Copenhagen Letter

- **Thesis:** The tech industry must **put humans before business**, treating people as citizens, not data points, and rejecting manipulation and growth-at-any-cost.
- **Mental models:** Tech as a **civic actor** that shapes democracy; creators as moral agents, not neutral builders.
- **Assumptions:** Business models and incentives are central to ethics; “scale” without care corrodes trust and society.

---

### Humane: A New Agenda for Tech – Center for Humane Technology

- **Thesis:** The core problem is **human downgrading**—the erosion of attention, mental health, and civic life by the attention economy. We must shift to a **trust economy** that optimizes for human strengths and flourishing.
- **Mental models:** Downgrading vs flourishing; attention economy vs trust economy; full-stack psychology used to support, not exploit, people.
- **Assumptions:** Tech is already deeply entangled with our minds and institutions; business models (ads, engagement) drive most harms.

---

### Tools for Conviviality – Ivan Illich

- **Thesis:** Distinguishes **convivial tools** (small, controllable, creativity-enhancing) from industrial tools that grow so large they dominate and reshape society (“radical monopolies”).
- **Mental models:** Convivial vs industrial tools; radical monopoly (e.g., cars reorganizing cities).
- **Assumptions:** Scale and complexity are moral variables; beyond a threshold, tools start using people instead of people using tools.

---

### Resonant Computing Manifesto

- **Thesis:** Current hyper-scale, centralized tech flattens context and alienates; **resonant computing** is an alternative: tools that adapt to the particularity of people and communities. Principles: **Private, Dedicated, Plural, Adaptable, Prosocial**.
- **Mental models:** “Hyper-scale logic” vs “scale in”; resonance as alignment with a person’s life; software as something that should leave you *more yourself*.
- **Assumptions:** AI can either amplify hyper-scale extraction or enable deeply personalized, human-scale tools, depending on architecture and incentives.

---

### Poetica: Manifesto for Humane App Design

- **Thesis:** Build what’s best for humans, not computers; treat collaboration as conversation; hide technical complexity behind **simple, flexible, intuitive** surfaces.
- **Mental models:** “Human Centered Engineering”; collaboration = good conversation; limited but expressive feature sets.
- **Assumptions:** Fewer, well-chosen interaction patterns can support richer collaboration than bloated feature sets; frictionless collaboration is a core human need.

---

### The Human-Centered AI Manifesto – Josh LaMar

- **Thesis:** AI must be designed to **enhance human flourishing** (individual, systemic, environmental) with principles like transparency, inclusivity, contextual adaptability, human–AI collaboration, and ethical data practices.
- **Mental models:** AI as a collaborator; flourishing as the evaluation metric; context-aware, human-in-the-loop systems.
- **Assumptions:** AI will be pervasive; only explicit commitments around data, representation, and accountability will keep it beneficial.

---

### The Manifesto of Society Centered Design

- **Thesis:** **Human-centered design is insufficient**; we must design for society, challenging exploitative capitalism and prioritizing care, rights, collective agency, and regenerative action.
- **Mental models:** From “user” to **society**; redistributing technological power; compassion at scale.
- **Assumptions:** Individual delight can mask systemic harm; ethical design must grapple explicitly with power and political economy.

---

### Manifesto for Human-Centered Technology Design – Dejan Markovic

- **Thesis:** Technology is a **medium for emotion and thought**, not an end. Start from empathy, design for emotional resonance and reduced friction, and bridge embodied human needs with clear digital experiences.
- **Mental models:** Emotional resonance as a success metric; interfaces as empathetic conversations.
- **Assumptions:** Emotional states are first-class design concerns; “efficient but alienating” is a failure mode.

---

### Human Centered Design: A Manifesto – Natalie Altman

- **Thesis:** Human-centered design is an **iterative, reflective practice** driven by questions: what story, what emotions, what MVP, and how do we learn quickly from real users?
- **Mental models:** Story framing; Wizard-of-Oz testing; MVP as a learning vehicle; reflection loops.
- **Assumptions:** Understanding emerges through cycles of making and testing; students and practitioners alike must center lived context, not assumptions.

---

### Principles of Humane Computing – Andrew Clement

- **Thesis:** Asks whether computers can **humanize society**, defining humane computing as tech that supports creative expression, communication, and mutual care, especially via small, inexpensive, community systems.
- **Mental models:** Humane vs alienating computing; community-oriented systems; principled criteria to judge “humaneness.”
- **Assumptions:** Size, cost, and locality matter; computing is not neutral—it either supports or undermines humanistic objectives.

---

### Calm Technology – Amber Case

- **Thesis:** Tech should require the **smallest possible amount of attention**, live mostly in the periphery, and inform without demanding; attention is finite and precious.
- **Mental models:** Center vs periphery of attention; calm vs noisy tech; multi-sensory, low-interruption signaling.
- **Assumptions:** Overload is default; good design is often about *less* interaction, not more.

---

### Magic Ink – Bret Victor

- **Thesis:** Most software is bureaucratic command interfaces; instead, **information software** should observe context, learn from behavior, and proactively show relevant information.
- **Mental models:** Command-driven vs context-driven software; software as rich visual medium; prediction and inference as core UI.
- **Assumptions:** Many user tasks can be anticipated; interaction cost should be minimized by intelligent, context-aware systems.

---

### The Humane Interface – Jef Raskin

- **Thesis:** A humane interface is responsive to human needs and frailties: avoid modes, reduce cognitive load, give strong undo, let the user set the pace.
- **Mental models:** “Cognetics” (interface + cognition); modelessness; quasimodes; monotony.
- **Assumptions:** Cognitive psychology should directly shape interface rules; most current UI complexity is gratuitous.

---

### Apples, Trees, and Quasimodes: On Humane Computing – System Stack

- **Thesis:** Places Raskin within the “tools for thought” lineage and argues that **commercial platforms structurally resist humane computing**, which thrives in smaller, fringe contexts.
- **Mental models:** Humane computing as counter-current to platform economics; quasimodes and predictability as liberatory patterns.
- **Assumptions:** You can’t bolt humane computing on top of misaligned incentives; organizational context is as important as UI craft.

---

### 7 Design Paradigms for Human–AI Interfaces

- **Thesis:** We need explicit paradigms for **visible, accountable AI**: accuracy controls, explanation-centered interaction, participatory customization, privacy-aware architecture, shared editable memory, robust error recovery, and visible value boundaries.
- **Mental models:** AI as **collaborator with knobs**; long-term memory as shared, editable space; explicit boundaries on what AI will/won’t do.
- **Assumptions:** Opaque “magic” undermines trust; people need control, legibility, and recourse when AI errs.

---

### Tools for Thought: Science, Design, Art, Craftsmanship? – Andy Matuschak

- **Thesis:** Progress in “tools for thought” comes from **insight through making** at the interface between cognition and artifact, blending science, design, art, and craft.
- **Mental models:** Tools for thought as a craft discipline; new abstractions as inventions; cognitive psychology as raw material.
- **Assumptions:** Transformative tools demand long-term iterative work; impact is measured in changes to thinking, not downloads.

---

### How Can We Develop Transformative Tools for Thought? – Nielsen & Matuschak

- **Thesis:** Most tools are incremental; we want **transformative** ones that qualitatively change understanding. Use in-the-wild experiments (e.g., mnemonic media) to evaluate and refine.
- **Mental models:** Transformative vs incremental tools; field experiments; measuring changes in comprehension and creativity.
- **Assumptions:** We can and should empirically test whether tools meaningfully change cognition.

---

### What is the IndieWeb? – IndieWeb principles

- **Thesis:** You should **own your domain and content**, publishing on your own site instead of platforms; your site is your online home.
- **Mental models:** “Own your turf”; POSSE (Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere); platforms as guests, not landlords.
- **Assumptions:** Personal sovereignty online is possible and desirable; federation and open standards can replace captive feeds.

---

### A Brief History & Ethos of the Digital Garden – Maggie Appleton

- **Thesis:** **Digital gardens** are evolving, interlinked personal knowledge spaces—non-linear, imperfect, and exploratory—contrasting with time-ordered social feeds.
- **Mental models:** Garden vs stream; notes as living, revisitable entities; growth over performance.
- **Assumptions:** People benefit from spaces where they can think in public, slowly and iteratively, outside performance metrics.

---

### Small Technology & the “Small Web” – Small Technology Foundation

- **Thesis:** Advocate **everyday tools for everyday people**: small, comprehensible, privacy-respecting software on personal or simple infrastructure; critique speculative Web3 in favor of a “small web.”
- **Mental models:** Small vs big tech; web0 vs web3; personal servers as toasters-not-mainframes.
- **Assumptions:** Simplicity, local control, and legibility are preconditions for ethical tech; complexity and scale drive opacity and abuse.

---

### The Internet with a Human Face – Maciej Cegłowski

- **Thesis:** Critiques the **centralized, surveillance web**; argues that permanent data storage and big-data logic create structural abuse and that “the only safe data is the data you don’t store.”
- **Mental models:** Natural forgetting vs permanent memory; central points of failure; surveillance capitalism.
- **Assumptions:** Data will be misused if it can be; ephemeral, decentralized systems better match human social expectations.

---

### Dark Patterns & Addictive Design

- **Thesis:** Documents how interfaces exploit cognitive biases—dark patterns, addictive loops, obscured consent—to extract value from users.
- **Mental models:** Pattern catalog of manipulation; “deceptive design” as a concept.
- **Assumptions:** UI is never neutral; if aligned with the wrong metrics, it becomes an instrument of exploitation.

## 2. Connections, overlaps, and tensions

### Major shared themes

1. **Flourishing & dignity as the goal**

   - Humane Tech, Human-Centered AI, Society Centered Design, Markovic, Altman, Clement, and the Resonant Computing Manifesto all say: the success of tech is measured in **human flourishing**—psychological, social, and civic—not just efficiency or engagement.
   - Ethical Design Manifesto and Copenhagen Letter connect flourishing to **rights and responsibility**: privacy, autonomy, non-manipulation.

2. **Convivial tools and human-scale ecosystems**

   - Illich, Clement, Small Tech, IndieWeb, Digital Gardens, and Resonant Computing share a **“small, plural, local”** ethos: tools should be understandable, controllable, and tailored to communities.
   - They resist radical monopolies and centralized platforms (Illich, Cegłowski, Society Centered Design), arguing for **plural, interoperable ecosystems**.

3. **Attention, cognition, and emotional resonance**

   - Calm Technology, Raskin, System Stack, Magic Ink, Markovic, and dark-pattern critiques all focus on **cognitive and emotional alignment**: respect limited attention, avoid modes and surprises, design for comfort and competence.
   - Human downgrading (Humane Tech) and addictive patterns describe the failure modes when attention and emotion are exploited.

4. **New interaction paradigms & human–AI collaboration**

   - Magic Ink, 7 AI paradigms, Poetica, Matuschak/Nielsen, and human-centered AI manifestos highlight patterns where tech is a **collaborator**, not a black box or bureaucrat: explanation, shared memory, error recovery, conversation-like interaction.
   - Tools for thought work (Matuschak, Nielsen) and digital gardens show how interaction patterns can **change how people think**.

5. **Practice, craft, and governance**

   - Altman, Matuschak, Nielsen, and the manifestos emphasize **iterative, reflective practice**: insight through making, early testing, field evaluation.
   - Society Centered Design, Copenhagen Letter, Humane Tech, and Human-Centered AI stress **governance and power**: business models, oversight, redistribution of tech power.

### Tensions and open questions

- **Individual vs societal focus**

  - Human-centered design and emotional resonance sometimes push toward frictionless, delightful experiences. Society Centered Design warns that frictionless individual delight can **mask systemic harm** (e.g., convenient surveillance).
  - Resolution in RCDS: treat **societal impact as a higher-level constraint**—if a delightful feature undermines rights or justice, it fails.

- **Automation/proactivity vs agency**

  - Magic Ink and predictive UIs want software to anticipate needs and reduce explicit commands. Humane Tech, 7 paradigms, and dark-pattern critiques warn that opaque proactivity can slip into **manipulation**.
  - Resolution: proactivity is acceptable when **transparent, controllable, and grounded in explicit user goals**, with visible boundaries and easy overrides.

- **Personalization vs privacy**

  - Resonant Computing, Magic Ink, tools for thought, and digital gardens want deeply personalized systems; Cegłowski, Ethical Design, and Human-Centered AI insist on **data minimalism and strong privacy**.
  - Resolution: favor **local-first, user-held context** (private/dedicated), ephemeral or encrypted data, and shared, editable memory under the user’s control—not centralized profiling.

## 3. The Resonant Computing Design Stack (RCDS)

We can now integrate everything into a coherent framework you can actually use.

### 3.1 Overview

**RCDS** is a four-layer model:

1. **Flourishing & Care (Ends)** – What the system is *for*.
2. **Structures & Scale (Incentives & Architecture)** – How it’s organized and funded.
3. **Attention & Interaction (Experience)** – How it behaves in front of humans.
4. **Practice & Governance (Process)** – How it’s built, evaluated, and steered over time.

Think of it as a stack you must align **top-to-bottom**. A resonant tool only really works if *all* layers support one another; misalignment (e.g., humane interface on top of surveillance business model) produces cognitive dissonance and eventual harm.

---

### 3.2 Layer 1 – Flourishing & Care (Ends)

**Core idea:** The purpose of the system is to foster **human and societal flourishing**, not just engagement or profit.

- **Principles**

  - **Human dignity and rights first**

    - From Ethical Design Manifesto, Copenhagen Letter, Human-Centered AI, Society Centered Design.
    - Non-negotiables: privacy, consent, non-manipulation, accessibility.
  - **From human downgrading to human upgrading**

    - From Humane Tech: design to *reduce* anxiety, polarization, and distraction; *increase* clarity, connection, and capability.
  - **Societal and ecological care**

    - From Society Centered Design, Human-Centered AI: consider communities, marginalized groups, and environment as first-class stakeholders.

- **How this guides decisions**

  - You treat every design choice as a bet on **what kind of person and society** the tool encourages.
  - “Will this feature leave people feeling more grounded, coherent, and connected?” is a central design question.

---

### 3.3 Layer 2 – Structures & Scale (Incentives & Architecture)

**Core idea:** The shape of the infrastructure and business model determines what’s even possible at the interface.

- **Principles**

  - **Convivial, human-scale tools**

    - From Illich, Clement, Small Tech, Resonant Computing: keep systems small enough to understand and adjust; avoid radical monopolies.
    - Prefer **Private, Dedicated, Plural** architectures (Resonant Computing): user-held context; multiple tools, not one empire.
  - **Data minimalism and local-first**

    - From Cegłowski, Ethical Design, IndieWeb, Digital Gardens: collect minimal data; store it ephemerally and locally when possible.
    - “The only safe data is data you don’t store” becomes a design constraint.
  - **Trust-aligned incentives**

    - From Copenhagen Letter, Humane Tech, Society Centered Design: business models should reward long-term trust, not short-term engagement or extraction.

- **How this guides decisions**

  - You choose **architectures** (local-first, user-owned domains, interoperable standards) and **business models** (subscriptions, co-ops, public funding) that don’t require manipulation to survive.
  - You design for **scale-in**: making a single community’s experience deep, rich, and trustworthy, not just chasing MAUs.

---

### 3.4 Layer 3 – Attention & Interaction (Experience)

**Core idea:** Interfaces should respect cognitive limits, emotional states, and agency, creating a sense of competence and calm.

- **Principles**

  - **Calm and cognitively aligned**

    - From Calm Technology, Raskin, System Stack: minimize interruption, avoid modes, make undo universal, keep mental models predictable.
    - Users set the pace; the system adapts to humans, not vice versa.
  - **Resonant emotional tone**

    - From Markovic, Altman, Poetica: design interfaces like humane conversations—clear, empathetic, low-friction but not manipulative.
    - Measure success partly by how people *feel* after using the tool.
  - **Context-aware, but transparent**

    - From Magic Ink, 7 AI paradigms, Human-Centered AI, Resonant Computing: software can infer and anticipate—but must show why, expose controls, and respect boundaries.
    - Shared editable memory; visible confidence; robust error recovery; visible value boundaries.

- **How this guides decisions**

  - You design notifications that live mostly in the periphery, with **explicit user controls**.
  - You avoid dark patterns (deceptive flows, hidden options) and addictive loops; instead, you implement **strong undo, clear exits, and honest defaults**.
  - For AI features, you expose **knobs and explanations** rather than a sealed “oracle.”

---

### 3.5 Layer 4 – Practice & Governance (Process)

**Core idea:** Resonant systems are built through **craft, iteration, and participatory governance**, not once-off genius.

- **Principles**

  - **Insight through making & experimentation**

    - From Matuschak, Nielsen, Altman: prototype early; test in the wild; measure cognitive and emotional impact, not just clicks.
    - Treat tools for thought as a long-term craft.
  - **Participatory, inclusive design**

    - From Human-Centered AI, Society Centered Design, Altman: include diverse stakeholders; invite users into customization and feedback (participatory customization in AI).
  - **Ongoing evaluation of “resonance”**

    - From Resonant Computing, Humane Tech, Clement: establish criteria like autonomy, clarity, connection, and conviviality; periodically review whether the tool still supports them.

- **How this guides decisions**

  - You plan for **continuous learning**: telemetry that tracks meaningful outcomes (time reclaimed, understanding gained), plus qualitative feedback (journals, interviews).
  - You design **governance mechanisms**: transparent policies, appeal and redress for harms, avenues for user voice in product direction.

## 4. Using the RCDS in practice

### 4.1 What to do more of (patterns)

1. **Design from flourishing backwards**

   - Start any project by writing a “flourishing brief”:

     - *What capacities do we want to expand?* (e.g., calm focus, deep understanding, stronger relationships.)
     - *For whom?* (individuals, communities, specific marginalized groups.)
   - Map each feature to a specific flourishing outcome. If you can’t, reconsider it.

2. **Choose convivial architectures**

   - Default to:

     - **Local-first, user-held data** (IndieWeb, Small Tech, Cegłowski, Resonant Computing).
     - **Plural tools** that interoperate (Plural principle; IndieWeb; digital gardens) instead of one mega-platform.
   - Give people clear paths to **export, fork, or self-host**.

3. **Make attention a protected resource**

   - Apply Calm Technology + Raskin:

     - Use periphery-friendly signals; make interruptions rare, meaningful, and user-configurable.
     - Ensure every action is reversible; minimize modes; keep mental models stable.
   - Periodically ask: *Could we accomplish this goal with fewer notifications and less on-screen clutter?*

4. **Treat AI as a transparent collaborator**

   - Implement 7 paradigms + Human-Centered AI:

     - Expose **confidence and accuracy controls**.
     - Offer **explanations** and provenance on demand.
     - Maintain **shared, editable memory** that users can inspect and prune.
     - Provide **robust error recovery** and clear boundaries on what the AI will/won’t do.
   - Measure success in **trust + capability**, not just usage frequency.

5. **Work like a craftsperson-researcher**

   - Use the tools-for-thought methodology:

     - Run **small, focused experiments** on whether your tool genuinely improves understanding or reduces cognitive load.
     - Use digital-garden-like spaces for design notes and ongoing reflection.
   - Incorporate **Wizard-of-Oz tests, MVPs, and user storytelling** (Altman) early to align with lived context.

---

### 4.2 What to avoid (anti-patterns & failure modes)

1. **Business models that reward addiction or surveillance**

   - Engagement-only KPIs that push you towards dark patterns or attention hijacking.
   - Centralized, indefinite data hoarding; aggregation of detailed behavioral logs "just in case."
   - “Free” products where the real currency is tracking and profile-building.

2. **Interface-level manipulation**

   - Dark patterns: disguised ads, pre-ticked checkboxes, confusing opt-outs, obstructive cancellation flows.
   - Slot-machine UX: infinite scroll, variable rewards, “streaks” that punish disengagement.
   - Mode-heavy, unpredictable interfaces that constantly surprise or trap users.

3. **Opaque AI and magical thinking**

   - AI features with no explanation, no way to correct or challenge output, and no visibility into *what they know about you*.
   - Long-term memory that can’t be inspected, edited, or reset.
   - Features that change behavior significantly without signaling new capabilities or boundaries.

4. **Radical monopolies and central points of failure**

   - Architectures where one provider **must** see and control everything (single giant server, proprietary lock-in, closed APIs).
   - Social or civic functions that depend on a single, unaccountable system for coordination.

5. **Token human-centricity**

   - “User delight” that ignores who pays the cost (e.g., workers, communities, environment).
   - Checklists and ethics statements without structural changes (e.g., surveillance businesses adopting “humane” UI skins).

---

### 4.3 How it changes design, build, and evaluation

If you adopt the **Resonant Computing Design Stack**, your workflow changes roughly like this:

1. **During strategy & discovery**

   - You explicitly frame the project in terms of **flourishing outcomes and societal impact** (Layer 1).
   - You select **architectures and business models** (Layer 2) that could plausibly support those outcomes without hidden contradictions.

2. **During design & implementation**

   - You treat **attention, cognition, and emotional resonance** as non-negotiable constraints (Layer 3): calm by default, strong undo, no dark patterns, transparent AI.
   - You design human–AI interactions around **visibility, agency, and shared memory**, not “wow” moments.

3. **During iteration & governance**

   - You embed **experimental evaluation** (Layer 4): measure whether the tool actually improves understanding, focus, or connection, not just usage.
   - You set up **feedback channels and governance** that allow users and affected communities to steer future changes.

When in doubt, you can literally run a product or feature through the stack:

- **Flourishing & Care:** Who is better off if this works? Who might be worse off?
- **Structures & Scale:** What incentives push this feature toward exploitation? How can we realign (e.g., pricing, hosting, ownership)?
- **Attention & Interaction:** Does this respect attention, reveal its assumptions, and make people feel competent?
- **Practice & Governance:** How will we know if this is downgrading or upgrading people’s lives six months from now? Who gets to say?

If those answers aren’t satisfying, you have a clear map of what to change to move closer to truly **resonant computing**.
